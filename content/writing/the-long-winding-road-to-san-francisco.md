There are few things I love more than taking Northbound I-85 up towards Atlanta and watching the glittering skyline unfold in front of me. It's a neatly packaged, vaguely spiritual amalgamation of millions of people and their accompanying struggles. "Here we are!," it says, "and you are a part of this!"

Also parts of this are the billboards that flank the highway, advertising diabetic supply exchanges, injury lawyers galore, and other high-margin perversities. And though the golden spires atop the 60-story high-rises hint at the exploitation and accumulation underlying it all, they require some causal inference. You need only be able to read to interpret the billboards.

They tell you that they'll sue the sleepless trucker who hit you and win, because you really need the money. They tell you, driver, that you are entering — or have always been a part of — a deeply sick society.

## The Median San Francisco Billboard

Something I love just as much as taking I-85 into Atlanta is taking 101-North into San Francisco. It's quite nice to see the shining city on a hill in the bay by the golden gates and briefly convince yourself that this is the Divine Order Of All Things. The billboards here are less bleak, and frankly just irritating.

> San Francisco is an incredibly wealthy city — per capita, at least, likely home to the greatest accumulated wealth in the history of humanity.

Over the last 70 years, ostensibly liberal people accumulated this wealth at a tremendous pace. These people supported racial equality, but accepted and then codified class-based discrimination, oftentimes by holding the government and its revenue hostage to the demands of homeowners.

Since 1990, housing and pretty much everything else have become inaccessible to all but the wealthiest in the Bay Area. The cost of living in the Bay Area is untenable, and the government cannot presently do anything about it because some of its more critical regulatory functions have been captured by homeowner interests and techie libertarianism.
